Introduction

The concept of Internet service providers providing all content and applications regardless of the source, and without any kind of discrimination to any particular product or website is called net neutrality. This is a complex issue and is related with social, political ethical and historical contexts. In this article, I am going to describe some concepts related to net neutrality, some of the common ways of hindering net neutrality , discuss some political positions regarding net neutrality, give an overview of some important figures and cases related to net neutrality and lastly discuss the ethical implications of this concept.

1. Concepts

Common Carrier

A common carrier, in telecommunications, is an entity that provides wired and wireless communication services to the general public for a fee. A common carrier can be contrasted with a contract carrier, also called a private carrier, which provides services to a limited number of customers.
Because common carriers serve the general public, they are licensed by a regulatory body. In the United States, the common carrier designation made by the Federal Communications Commission (FCC), under authorization of the Telecommunications Act of 1934. One reason common carriers are licensed is to ensure they provide indiscriminate public access and protect the privacy of the general public. Whether or not the FCC has the legal right to declare that internet service providers (ISPs) are common carriers is a major consideration in the net neutrality debate. [1]

Communication Service vs. Utility

So to ensure net neutrality by law, it boils down to whether internet is defined as a common carrier or not. In 2002, the Federal communications Commission classified broadband internet providers as Information Services (Title I) which allowed relaxed regulations of the internet. But in 2015, Tom Wheeler, chairman of FCC reclassified broadband providers as a public utility under Title II to enforce open internet order and net neutrality. So the debate becomes whether broadband should be considered as a communication service or utility. There are several interesting cases regarding this which will be discussed below.

2. Types of neutrality and control

Source: http://www.cm-life.com/article/2017/11/net-neutrality-editorial

Before we go into the debate on internet regulation, let us first see how the internet can be controlled. Some of the common ways are traffic shaping, blocking p2p file sharing, data discrimination by protocol, peering etc. I am going to discuss two of these methods below:

Traffic shaping

Traffic shaping, also known as "packet shaping," is the practice of regulating network data transfer to assure a certain level of performance, quality of service (QoS) or return on investment (ROI). The practice involves delaying the flow of packets that have been designated as less important or less desired than those of prioritized traffic streams.[2]

Blocking P2P file sharing

P2P file sharing is a technology for efficient sharing of data between peers across the Internet. Just as with any other technology for transferring files, P2P file sharing can be used for sharing lawful as well as unlawful content. There is a great deal of lawful content, such as open-licensed software and digital media, that can be downloaded through P2P file sharing. The reason many ISPs block P2P traffic is because more than half of the traffic on the Internet is P2P traffic and a small group of active P2P users can typically use up the majority of an ISPs available bandwidth. 3. Political viewpoints

The net neutrality issue can be considered differently according to the different social and economic structures. Some prominent social and economic positions are: neo-liberalism, conservatism, libertarianism, laissez-faire capitalism, free market economy etc. I am going to discuss two of these political standpoints and see how the net neutrality issue is interpreted in the two socio-economic structure.

Neo-liberalism

Neoliberalism is a term for different social and economic ideas. Originally the term was used by a group of liberals who helped shape social market economy in the mid-20th century. Neoliberalism is characterized by free market trade, deregulation of financial markets, individualisation, and the shift away from state welfare provision. Increasingly, the market fails to yield fair or equitable outcomes. In the application of neoliberal ideals, the state might also intervene and regulate, for example, to prevent exploitation, or to ensure social fairness and equality. [4]
So in this standpoint, the govenrment has the right to regulate internet service providers and put laws so that net neutrality is achieved.

Laissez-faire capitalism

Source: https://marketbusinessnews.com/financial-glossary/laissez-faire-definition-meaning/

Laissez-faire economics is a theory that restricts government intervention in the economy. It holds that the economy is strongest when all the government does is protect individuals' rights. Laissez-faire is French for "let do." In other words, let the market do its own thing. If left alone, the laws of supply and demand will efficiently direct the production of goods and services. Supply includes natural resources, capital, and labor. Demand includes purchases by consumers, businesses, and the government. [5] So it is seen that, Laissez-faire economy doesn't support government regulation on internet service providers. It is the markets decision to find out if net neutrality is necessary or not.

Tom Wheeler: Pro Net Neutrality

President Barack Obama appointed Tom Wheeler as chair of the Federal Communications Commission in 2013. On his watch, the FCC passed the Open Internet Order that established net neutrality and reclassified broadband internet providers as "common carriers" similar to landline and cellular phone providers. Wheeler had spent time during the 1980s as CEO of a tech startup called NABU, which offered an AOL-like home computer networking service that used cable television lines. That experience helped him understand the importance of open networks.[6]
